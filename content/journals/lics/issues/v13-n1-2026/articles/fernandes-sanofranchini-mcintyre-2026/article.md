# INTRODUCTION 

Between labor strikes by data workers in Kenya and community organizing in United States cities like Memphis and Tucson to halt and rollback toxic data centers, increasing attention has been drawn to the relationship among "artificial intelligence," the tech industry, and the global centralization of power. Jasmina Tacheva and Srividya Ramasubramanian describe the ever-increasing global power and influence of artificial intelligence as "AI Empire," a term that has since been echoed by Karen Hao and Nick Srnicek, among others. Following a long line of imperial progress narratives, AI Empire is built on the "ongoing legacy of colonialism and its ideology of racial and technological supremacy," as well as "the various forms of the extraction and appropriation of social resources through data" (Hao 5). In particular, Tacheva and Ramasubramanian note that "the interconnected systems of heteropatriarchy, racial capitalism, white supremacy, and coloniality converge in AI Empire's mechanisms of extractivism, automation, essentialism, surveillance, and containment" (6). AI Empire, then, echoes empires of the past, keeping in mind how resource and labor extraction, essentialism, surveillance, and containment have always been central to colonial projects (Spurr). However, AI Empire operates under the rule of multinational technology companies promising utopian futures facilitated by "artificial intelligence" made possible through the exploited labor of neocolonial subjects.

This article traces the rhetorical contours of AI Empire with a focus on how generative AI adoption in higher education and beyond often relies on what we refer to as "critwashing," a form of "ethics washing" (Bietti) and a rhetorical strategy that facilitates the appearance of critical engagement through appeals to diversity, equity, inclusion, and access discourses at the same time that discussions of concrete harms are negated. We argue that critwashing---which we discuss in further detail below---obfuscates extractivism and labor exploitation of data workers, among other harms associated with the tech industry. As we do so, we discuss, critique, and question current discourses about the relationship between generative AI and marginalized writers and communities. For example, how does understanding the climate crisis as a mass-disabling event complicate claims that generative AI is useful for neurodivergent writers? How will we grapple with the environmental implications of generative AI, especially given recent reports about xAI's supercomputer Colossus, which powers Elon Musk's generative AI chatbot Grok, and its 35 portable methane gas generators that spew smog and haze over the predominantly Black South Memphis community ("Joint Statement"; Kerr)? How do broad discourses and even recommendations about how generative AI can be used to facilitate equity and accessibility for "marginalized writers" become a way to "all lives matter" our way out of serious consideration of generative AI ethics? How are ethics and accessibility discourses used to insulate the tech industry from criticism of their practices and products? Whose voices have been invited into the conversation about the impact of generative AI, including its development, use, and continuation? Whose voices have been excluded and why? How have critwashing tactics shaped decisions about generative AI both in education and beyond? By elaborating on the rhetorical strategy of critwashing, we hope to equip readers with literacies that recognize how global labor (and other ethical problems associated with generative AI) are erased to promote "ethical" and "responsible" AI adoption.

Much like other forms of colonialism, AI Empire requires imperialists---this time in the form of tech companies---to invent a version of the colonial subject that they can claim will be helped and enriched by the colonizers' theft of their land, resources, and labor, similar to the "civilizing mission" that has motivated long histories of colonization and conquest. As Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak notes, the colonial subject ("the Other") is produced by the colonizers themselves to reinforce the colonizer as the default authority on the lives, experiences, and needs of the colonized. This production of the Other, and their accompanying silencing, is part of what Spivak calls "territorial imperialism," which is when

> a group of countries, generally first-world, are in the position of investing capital; another group, generally third-world, provide the field for investment, both through the comprador indigenous capitalists and through their ill-protected and shifting labor force. In the interest of maintaining the circulation and growth of industrial capital (and of the concomitant task of administration within nineteenth-century territorial imperialism), transportation, law and standardized education systems were developed---even as local industries were destroyed, land distribution was rearranged, and raw material was transferred to the colonizing country (83).

This process of territorial imperialism requires the enforced silence of the actual humans harmed by imperialist processes because allowing them to speak disrupts narratives about the largesse and benevolence of the colonizer and the benefits supposedly imputed upon colonized communities and peoples. In the case of AI Empire (as we will see below), this silence is often enforced through economic and legal threats in the form of lost wages, threats to workers' positions, and non-disclosure agreements.

In the sections that follow, we describe our own personal entanglements with digital damage (Edwards) and then discuss and analyze stories from the Distributed Artificial Intelligence Research (DAIR) Institute's Data Workers Inquiry, which positions data workers as community research and experts (Adio-Adet et al.). We argue that these multimodal narratives offer an important response to generative AI "critwashing"---or the act of using social justice language to ethically sanitize the use of generative AI---in writing studies and higher education more broadly. As part of this discussion, we position the Data Workers' Inquiry as one way of pushing back on stock arguments that support the adoption and integration of generative AI technologies through critwashing. Finally, we close by connecting these data workers' narratives to socially just pedagogies in rhetoric, composition, and literacy classrooms. Ultimately, we argue that just pedagogies require honesty and action about the current and ongoing harms, including labor exploitation, that come from the growth of generative AI technologies and the associated AI Empire to which they belong.

# PERSONAL ENTANGLEMENTS IN DIGITAL DAMAGE AND EMPIRE 

Cultural rhetoricians have articulated the importance of theorizing relationality and accountability in rhetorical investigations into the environment and empire (Ríos; Edwards; Cobos et al.). Dustin Edwards, for example, argues that "[a]ttending to digital damage through such a relational understanding helps bring to light relations that are easily obscured by cultural imaginaries of digital infrastructures" (64). Edwards' work takes the conversation about digital damage beyond a demand for (or performance of) moral purity, arguing instead that storying digital damage presents "a mode of increasing sensitivity to and *accountabilities* for the fraught entanglements and uneven conditions of life in the Anthropocene" (69). And so, we reflect on our entanglements in these unethical systems both to state our limitations and to make commitments to being responsible for our actions and accountable to our local and global communities. We also recognize this attention to our entanglements as one way of resisting critwashing. As we write and work together, we use cloud-based technologies including Google Docs, Gmail, Outlook, and Zoom, all of which are supported by university licensing. As three writing scholars working at two large, public, research intensive, land grant state universities, our work is linked to specific institutionally-authorized technologies and information environments that consume great amounts of land, energy, and water (Noble and Roberts). In addition, we have access to research support, resources, and materials that others might not and that is made possible in conjunction with different parts of our respective university systems, including those in non-tenure track, teaching-intensive roles at our institutions. Of course, this inventory does not account for the various ways we use resource intensive digital technologies in our personal lives when we doomscroll on social media, binge watch series on various platforms, or hoard images in the cloud, to name a few areas for improvement.

By acknowledging the ways in which we are entangled in AI Empire and digital damage, we can identify moments for refusal when they are possible (Fernandes et al.; Sano-Franchini et al.). Over the course of our time writing, we confronted our reliance on Zoom and made conscientious efforts to make our collaborations less resource-intensive by pivoting to three-way phone calls. Still, we cannot fully extricate our work and writing from an unethical supply chain. "There is no ethical consumption under capitalism" has become a common truism, almost a throwaway statement. Similarly, land acknowledgements have been criticized as performative and empty statements. What is an acknowledgment without action? The question is a fair one. In this way, we hope to demonstrate that what we are arguing is not a question of moral purity; regardless of one's various ethical entanglements, we can---and should---still have opportunities to make ethical choices to resist oppressive technologies. That we are complicit in harm is no reason to suggest that we should not take steps to reduce that harm. Reckoning with the need to divest from oppressive technology---for personal and professional work---is an important step to take, even if one is not immediately in the position to do so. It is a serious indictment of the oppressive tech we discuss that opting out is so difficult, and we need to name this difficulty without offloading individual responsibility. Moreover, it is important to recognize how discourses of moral purity are ultimately rhetorical barriers that blockade the option or need for taking action in the direction of social justice when and where one can do so. Visibility---of choices and of labor---is vital to building better, more just futures.

# THE DISTRIBUTED ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE RESEARCH (DAIR) INSTITUTE'S DATA WORKERS' INQUIRY AS STORYING AI EMPIRE

The Distributed Artificial Intelligence Research (DAIR) Institute's Data Workers' Inquiry project represents one effort to counter the intentional invisibilization of data workers. Critical to the everyday operation of large, multinational tech corporations and the development and deployment of AI, data workers annotate, label, and tag tons and tons of data to train and improve AI technologies. On social media platforms, data workers carry out content moderation processes by monitoring, reviewing, and filtering user- and now LLM-generated content. In this section, we center the voices and expertise of data workers by analyzing stories from the Distributed Artificial Intelligence Research (DAIR) Institute's Data Workers Inquiry, a community-based research project that positions data workers as community research and experts (Adio-Adet et al.). As DAIR notes in their 2022 *Research Philosophy*,

> DAIR's research is intended to benefit communities which are typically not served by AI and create pathways to refuse, interrogate, and reshape AI technologies together. We strive for research which is comprehensive---incorporating ways of knowing which consider lived experience and methods which move beyond a falsely objective analysis, and take people into account by refusing to trust quick technical solutions (2).

The Data Workers Inquiry, therefore, introduces the workers themselves as experts on their own experiences, on the impact of these technologies, and on the corporations that develop, deploy, and profit from them. This project---which takes the form of multimodal narratives---is one way of pushing back on the kinds of silencing inherent to AI Empire through story. Like other works that document the effects of AI Empire and data work on workers (Crawford; Hao; Miceli and Posada; Roberts; Williams et al.), the Data Workers' Inquiry use of storytelling counters the systematic erasure of data workers' labor by the tech industry and therefore is useful in efforts to combat critwashing discourses, which function to obscure that labor behind the mask of instantaneous automation. We do not understand the authors of these pieces as mere "victims"; instead, these data workers have a lot to teach us about their experiences as they demonstrate "technological ingenuity, alterity, sustained resistances, and refusals against the dehumanizing logics of AI Empire" (Tacheva and Ramasubramanian 2).

DAIR shares stories from data workers working from and living in several countries across three continents, including Kenya, Colombia, Germany, Syria, Lebanon, and Brazil. It is notable that many of these narratives are presented in English, reflecting the global dominance of mainstream English, though many of the multimodal narratives are presented both in the writer's language and English. The Data Workers Inquiry is divided into four sections: 1) Data Workers in Kenya Organize; 2) Syrian Data Workers in their own Words; 3) Latin American Data Workers Say Enough!' and 4) Content moderators in Germany speak out. In this essay, we analyze four of the data workers' stories---one from each of the four sections---to draw attention to how AI companies function as forms of corporate empire spanning multiple continents. The four stories we focus on are:

1.  "Click Captives. The Unseen Struggle of Data Workers" by Wilington Shitawa

2.  "Artificial AI" by Ruba

3.  \"Annotate to Educate: The Dual Life of a Syrian Student & Data Annotator\" by Yasser Yousef Alrayes

4.  "Mind over Moderation" by Anonymous Workers (Doe and Joe)

Taken together, the stories offer a sense of the intimacies---to borrow the language of Lisa Lowe---of AI Empire; that is, the often-obscured connections and "less visible forms of alliance, affinity, and society among variously colonized peoples beyond the metropolitan center" (19). We additionally decided to focus on these four narratives as they enable us to include different aspects of data workers' exploitation in different contexts, whether in terms of place and regional histories and politics, in terms of work responsibilities and labor arrangements, or in terms of the companies involved. Further, this set of stories uses a range of modalities and narrative structures---including a comic, a day in the life video blog, an animation, and a podcast---that can be useful in a variety of pedagogical contexts; Maggie has specifically found multimodal narratives to be helpful illustrations in her undergraduate digital cultural rhetorics courses. In sum, these stories offer experiences of life across AI Empire and, taken together, highlight how this work intersects with globalization and border politics. These stories are also strong examples of works that counter the kinds of critwashing AI patterns that we discuss later in this piece.

## Wilington's "Click Captives: The Unseen Struggle of Data Workers"

"Click Captives: The Unseen Struggle of Data Workers" by Wilington Shitawa is a comic strip that exposes many of the problems associated with the "hidden labor" of data workers, including the low wages, long working hours, and the poor working conditions. This comic strip draws on Wilington's personal experiences and interviews with his colleagues Andrea, Derrick, and Edwin. The comic strip tells the story of two young friends, Wilington and Edwin, talking about their experiences working as data annotators and quality analysts at tech companies Cloud Factory and Sama in Nairobi, Kenya. In the introduction, Wilington describes this story as "a tale of exploitation, resilience, and the realities of being a data worker in the global supply chain of the AI industry" (4). In "Click Captives," Wilington and Edwin narrate their movement from the "slums of Nairobi" to the tech industry in Kenya to find their way out of poverty and achieve their dreams of financial stability. Their story begins with Wilington and Edwin reconnecting at a bar where they reflect on their time working first for Cloud Factory and then for Sama: "Honestly, I'm still trying to process everything we went through. The long hours, low pay, and constant fear of losing our jobs," Willington explains.

Their experience as data workers began optimistically. Edwin is pictured in the fourth panel, full of hope, thinking about a house, money, and a car: "I remember when we first started at Cloud Factory. We were so full of hope, thinking this would be our ticket out of the slums." In addition to feeling like data work could be the beginning of a stable career in tech, Cloud Factory representatives also promised employees that they had an important part to play in "the tech revolution," appealing to very common human desires for meaningful work. The reality of working for Cloud Factory, however, was not all it was promised to be. The friends describe working long hours that "could stretch to 11 hours or more." Even worse, they were not paid for working the extra hours---an insult to their dignity as human workers: "It was like our time and effort meant nothing to them." This reflection can also be read as an assertion of the disrespect and disregard they felt, not unlike the kinds of dehumanization that is part and parcel to empires of old. Not surprisingly, the friends describe feeling constant physical and mental exhaustion that "took a toll on [their] health and personal life," as well as a need to drink "just trying to cope with the stress of the job." What's more, they describe working in unstable and precarious conditions, with short contracts leaving them "in constant fear of losing [their] jobs," and making it impossible to "plan for the future or make any long-term commitments." The opportunities promised by Cloud Factory ultimately amounted to exploitation. After leaving Cloud Factory to work for Sama, another tech company, Wilington and Edwin found that they were "trapped in a cycle of exploitation," as they faced "the same projects, the same unrealistic targets, and the same disregard for our well-being."

This exploitation was made possible through strategic decisions to prey on workers in already-precarious situations to enable control and entrapment: "We were at the mercy of these companies. They knew we needed the work and they used that power to keep us in line, no matter how bad it got." Yet Wilington's story shows how although workers may quite reasonably feel like they have limited options for resisting the labor abuses of this industry, storytelling literacies and storytelling as a rhetorical activity can be a catalyst for resistance. Unwilling to be rendered powerless and to consider the future of these industries inevitable and outside of his control, Wilington's story concludes with a call for action: "It's time for these companies to be held accountable for their actions and for the well-being of their workers to be prioritized." Wilington calls for industry change, understanding that his personal story matters as a contributing factor that can enable global mobilization: "Many workers endure immense hardship and even depression, silently hoping for the world to take notice of their plight and take action."

## Yasser's "Annotate to Educate"

"Annotate to Educate: The Dual Life of a Syrian Student & Data Annotator" is an eight and half minute long short film about Yasser's life as a student and data worker. By day, Yasser attends the University of Damascus as a computer engineering student and works hard to study subjects that he is truly passionate about. In the evenings, he works as a data annotator. This vlog documents a typical day in Yasser's life, following him from the time he wakes up in his shared room until he takes the bus home from the bar. In this vlog, we see how Yasser's education and data work are made more difficult by expectations for self-reliance and resilience amidst insufficient data work training and resourcing.

Data annotation is time and resource intensive. It requires extensive training. Yasser often works with his friend, the two of them looking at one screen as they deliberate about word choices in the training process. To complete projects, they must watch lengthy training videos that are difficult to follow. The inefficiency of the training process is most notable when Yasser is tasked with annotating data far beyond his expertise, like images of wrist fractures that will be used to train AI for healthcare and medical industries: "To be part of this project, I needed more efficient training, which was hard to come by." Insufficient training consumes a great deal of Yasser's time; it increases the pressure on him to complete his daily tasks, and it jeopardizes his compensation.

Indeed, no matter how many hours or personal resources Yasser expends, Yasser notes, "the compensation is always subject to change at the end of the project." In one scene from his day, he points to a long list of tasks he completed over the course of ten hours that had been accepted by his supervisors. "Unfortunately," Yasser explains, clicking to another screen, "if you move to this page, I find out that the client rejected all of them, and I have to repeat the work from the beginning." Yasser earns nothing when companies reject his work, and in cases like this, data work often costs him money rather than helping him support himself and his education due to the cost incurred doing this work. Syria's frequent power outages add to the difficulties of Yasser's data work. "When the electricity cuts out in an instant, our world plunges into darkness, and our work is interrupted," Yasser explains in a voice over, as the video footage shows him and his friend walking through the old streets. He continues: "In order to finish our daily target tasks, we need to find a place in Damascus that has power and Internet." Public transportation in Syria is strained, with too few buses operating for too many people, and "adds to the daily struggles of the resilient Syrian people." Traveling to Internet cafes takes time and money, but Yasser makes do, uncertain whether he will make back the money he must spend to do his work.

After completing his data work for the day, Yasser returns to his studies before taking a bus with a friend out to a bar. He enjoys a drink and his time with friends but is careful to note that "It's quite amusing that I worked for seven hours today to earn six dollars and that I spent one of those dollars on a drink that I finished in just five minutes." The vlog closes with footage of Yasser sitting in the back of a bus on his way home from the bar, tired from a long day of hard work and "hoping for a better tomorrow." For Yasser, a better tomorrow would include "a well-structured training process [that] value[s] our time and hard work" and fair compensation for learning, including when mistakes are made.

## Ruba's "Artificial AI"

"Artificial AI" is a seven-minute-long film narrated in Portuguese about Ruba's experience as a data worker in Brazil. This piece focuses on the massive invisibilized labor force required to make generative AI tools possible. In doing so, it challenges the illusion of robotic automation that drives the marketing of AI: "Behind the supposedly autonomous AI tools that are supposed to revolutionize the world, there is a hidden reality and a huge workforce that makes this technology possible." Ruba also explains that the video "stems from the frustration of feeling cheated by the promise of autonomy."

The film opens with 8-bit electronic video game sounding music accompanied by applause and the sound of a crowd of people cheering. The music is somewhat foreboding in tone, and it builds up in fullness, creating a sense of anxious anticipation. Soon, a discordant, robotic sounding voice announces in Portuguese, "The era of huge, noisy and dangerous factories is over! You no longer have to go through a dull, exhausting, and repetitive routine to earn the salary you need to survive." This opening scene communicates the uneasy feeling that comes with this message of hope that one can level up. It also highlights the existing conditions and promises that might make data work seem appealing---the imagined white-collar comfort of sitting at a desk as opposed to the hardship of factory work. This point also speaks to how countries with high rates of poverty and unemployment are targeted as sources of cheap and exploitable labor by large multinational technology companies. As a result, poor and exploited populations of people are further exploited for being poor and exploited.

Ruba goes on to describe how "The workforce are called 'independent contractors' and are recruited with warm promises of good earnings and flexible hours to carry out tasks that are presented as so simple that it seems like a charity to pay someone for doing them. But these are demanding activities," where the pacing of work is monitored and tightly controlled and where "the pay is hilarious" and sometimes nonexistent, as it was in Wilington's story. What's more, Ruba notes that data workers are not allowed to talk about their work, having signed "extensive contracts and confidentiality terms" at the same time that online influencers "[tout] the wonders of working on AI projects run by these outsourcing platforms." As a result, the data workers' lived realities are erased and, even more insultingly, contradicted in online recruitment by exploitative companies. In this way, the contracts and confidentiality agreements function rhetorically within a larger ecology of exploitation that includes HR recruitment strategies to silence, negate, and further exploit data workers.

## Anonymous Workers (Doe and Joe)'s "Mind Over Moderation"

"Mind Over Moderation" is a 26-minute podcast episode, hosted by two content moderators located in Berlin, Germany. At the outset of the episode, "Doe" and "Joe" note that they've disguised their voices because they've signed non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) that prevent them from speaking openly about content moderation work they do, thus mirroring the silencing tactic that Ruba experienced. In the text on the DAIR website that accompanies the episode, Doe and Joe explain that they are moderators for "a large social media platform working through an outsourcing company in Berlin." This role is, they say, "precarious" and "makes [them] 'prisoners' of [their] circumstances" because their immigration status is tied to their employment and the specific work they do for the Big Tech contractor for whom they work.

Their goals for the podcast include making their work visible and calling attention to the inhumane conditions in which they work by casting light on "the hidden toll" this work takes on moderators. What exactly is that work? According to Doe and Joe, "We are mostly known as the cleaners of the platform...as the trash people of the digital world." They are tasked with "monitor[ing] and manag[ing] user-generated content" to "ensure compliance with community guidelines and legal standards" with platform rules around acceptable content: "We remove the dirty stuff...We see it so you don't have to." A typical day, they note, includes working alongside other moderators in a large, open room. The physical space, they say, does not make it easy to "stare into the abyss...[of] some of the darkest aspects of human behavior," which is how they characterize the reality of the work they're doing. These dark aspects of human behavior include everything from "hate speech," which they frame as as a "minor violation of the guidelines," to "self-inflicted harm and suicide imagery" as more extreme examples. Doe and Joe explain, "[T]he nature of the content can be extremely disturbing, and the regular exposure to such extreme content can be mentally and emotionally damaging."

Ostensibly, moderators are allowed to "abandon" their workstation at any point to protect their mental health, but Doe and Joe note that this policy is "only for appearances" because moderators' decisions to leave particular moderating tasks undone "are recorded and analyzed" and contribute to their average times, which are surveilled by managers and platforms. Moreover, benchmarks and expectations constantly change, and they can count on their supervisors to "always tell [them] to be faster." Meanwhile, their NDAs prevent them from talking to friends and family about the content they see or the work they do, which Doe and Joe affirm can be isolating and exacerbate their feelings of "hopelessness" and "emotional exhaustion." While their employer provides access to "life coaches" whose goal is to help them be "more resilient," effective and sustained mental health support is largely non-existent.

They see their work as necessary, but invisible and undervalued by both the platforms for whom they work and the users of those platforms---users who may not even be aware that they exist. As they note a few times, many users assume that moderation is automated even though human moderators are involved in many platform moderation decisions, including in the review of material that is especially harmful. Despite their precarity and invisibility, moderators are constantly "risking their safety, their mental health" to try to prevent harmful content on social media platforms. And yet, the outsourcing contractor they work for offers lower pay and far fewer benefits than companies like Meta and Google. At the same time, Big Tech companies continue to obscure the work they do, relying on users' belief that auto-moderation is more widespread and more capable than it is to keep hidden the labor exploitation of human moderators.

# ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION: UNDERSTANDING DATA WORKERS' STORIES THROUGH CRITICAL AND RHETORICAL SCHOLARSHIP ON EMPIRE

Across these stories, what emerges is a clear picture of systemic oppression and exploitation in the tech industry. The abuses experienced by data workers are not specific to any one tech company or any specific kind of position under the broad umbrella of data work. Additionally, we identify several themes across the narratives:

1.  A promise of opportunity for people who are in desperate need using rhetorics of professionalism ("independent contractor"), rendering images of easy white-collar work, and assurances of freedom and flexibility

2.  Low wages, inconsistent pay, and frequently withheld earnings at the discretion of companies and clients

3.  Unpredictable and irregular work schedules that prevent workers from pursuing other economic opportunities

4.  Surveillance and control, including via unreasonable timelines and quotas

5.  Invisibilization and erasure---the use of non-disclosure agreements and contracts to silence workers, inhibit coalition building, and enforce perceptions of instantaneous automation

6.  Dehumanization in the forms of disrespect from supervisors, impossible-to-follow rules, and impossible-to-meet quotas

7.  Entrapment, via lack of other economic opportunities and immigration realities that tie particular positions to legal status in a given country

8.  Individual resiliency and self-reliance in response to corporate unwillingness to provide necessary support and resources

Together, these stories make clear how these tech companies rely on exploitation in the hopes of generating shareholder profits. The cruelty is the point, as data workers like Ruba and Yasser complete hours of labor only to be denied compensation when the work is inexplicably said to be found wanting. It's no surprise that data workers understood their grueling realities to be rooted not in opportunities but in exploitation and oppression. As Doe and Joe note, promises of advancement and growth were quickly overwhelmed by their actual experiences of quotas and time pressures even as they suffered from depression and "emotional exhaustion." The hard truth is that it is they and their labor---the same labor that enables the continued existence of multi-billion-dollar companies---are intentionally obscured, undervalued, and exploited.

In some ways, Wilington's, Ruba's, and Doe and Joe's accounts are reminiscent of the much longer history of indentured Asian labor in the U.S. and British colonies, especially in the 1800s, where Chinese, Indian, and other workers were lured to the colonies with false promises of financial wellness and freedom only to find themselves locked into grueling conditions (Lee; Lowe). Citing Aníbal Quijano and Immanuel Wallerstein, Lisa Lowe recounts how "the invention of race and ethnicity in the Americas as shifting, flexible classifications of difference [was] devised for governing different peoples for labor extraction within the colonial division of labor" (31--32). Imperialism---whether in the form of AI Empire or otherwise---relies on racial classifications and immigration status to prop up the labor exploitation required to perpetuate its extractive project. That is, this distributed approach to empire and colonial conquest is part and parcel of longer histories of colonization, where "a racial taxonomy gradually emerged both to manage and modernize labor, reproduction, and society among the colonized, as well as to ... discipline the range of potential 'intimacies' among them" (Lowe 32). Racial (and class) divisions are necessary to prevent solidarity among colonial subjects, and solidarity is precisely the call we see across these Data Workers' narratives. As Doe and Joe argue, "Our hope is that many more content moderators will voice their contempt and collectively fight for fairer working conditions, as we can only fight the seeming omnipotence of tech giants by organizing and standing together."

What's more, the rhetoric of AI Empire mirrors the rhetoric of empires of the past insofar as promises of inclusivity and freedom are contingent on exclusion, theft, exploitation, and abuse. For instance, Lowe writes,

> Even as it proposes inclusivity, liberal universalism effects principles of inclusion and exclusion; in the very claim to define humanity, as a species or as a condition, its gestures of definition divide the human and the nonhuman ... liberal ideas of political emancipation, ethical individualism, historical progress, and free market economy were employed in the expansion of empire ... universalizing concepts of reason, civilization, and freedom effect colonial divisions of humanity, affirming liberty for modern man while subordinating the variously colonized and dispossessed peoples whose material labor and resources were the conditions of possibility for that liberty (6).

This dynamic is visible when we examine AI hype and AI marketing rhetoric that promises the freedom of more time through purported efficiency, supposed inclusion via access to the ability to produce outputs without cultivating the literacies typically required for artistic production, and ostensible progress through the unquestioned good that will result from technological development. Yet as Kate Crawford illustrates in *Atlas of AI*, these promises of saved time often cover over "the fact that people are often performing rote tasks to shore up the impressions that machines can do the work...large-scale computation is deeply rooted in and running on the exploitation of human bodies" (57). Importantly, these essential relations are seemingly negated through what Lowe refers to as "the economy of affirmation and forgetting"---geographical distance, racial division, and broad erasure of the fact that these promises of freedom and progress necessarily rely on the exploitation of colonized subjects and lands (3). The erasure and forced invisibility of data workers, as relayed by Wilington, Yasser, Ruba, and Doe and Joe, is a prerequisite for the continuation of AI Empire. Furthermore, this erasure is fundamental to what we will discuss in the next section as critwashing discourses of generative AI.

# APPLICATION: AGAINST CRITWASHING GENERATIVE AI

To say that there are many ethical problems with generative AI is an understatement. Recent scholarship within rhetoric and writing studies about generative AI has included attention to---and acknowledgment of---the ethical harms of generative AI, including its implications for the environment, labor within and beyond higher education, and language homogenization. In response, scholars have articulated "critical AI literacy" and "ethical use" as strategies to help students and future professionals understand this technology's implications for the environment, labor, and writing as a human act broadly (Frazier and Hensley; Thompson and Pokrel; MLA-CCCC "Student Guide"). For example, the MLA-CCCC Joint Task Force on Writing and AI's first working paper urged calm in the face of proliferating generative AI products and discussed potential risks for students, teachers, programs, and the profession as whole. Yet, since then, much of the conversation has treated the ethical challenges of generative AI as something to be acknowledged and moved on from, usually as a segue to lengthy perspectives about how to use generative AI products in "ethical," "critical," or "responsible" ways. The MLA-CCCC Joint Task Force advocated, too, that "educators should draw on our collective expertise to shape how generative AI is used"; the question of whether it should be used at all is not taken up, even as ethical challenges and harms are acknowledged throughout the Task Force's three Working Papers. Such approaches are often premised upon the inevitability of these technologies and their impacts on the future of work and student learning.

While these responses are understandable in the face of an intense push for generative AI integration, we argue that pedagogies that foreground "critical AI literacy" based on "ethical use" are a form of what we refer to as "critwashing" and what has been called "critical washing" by Marcela Suarez Estrada, Barbara C.N. Muller, Olivia Guest, and Iris Van Rooij. For us, "critwashing" is a rhetorical strategy that facilitates the appearance of critical engagement through appeals to diversity, equity, inclusion, and access discourses while discussions of concrete harms are negated. We understand critwashing as a specific form of "ethics washing," which Elettra Bietti describes as one way that Big Tech seeks to avoid regulation and oversight while simultaneously bolstering their reputations with consumers. Bietti explains,

> On one hand, [ethics] has been used by companies as an acceptable façade that justifies deregulation, self-regulation or market driven governance, and is increasingly identified with technology companies' self-interested adoption of appearances of ethical behavior. Such growing instrumentalization of ethical language by tech companies has been called 'ethics washing.' Beyond AI ethics councils or AI Ethics researchers, the ethics washing critique extends to corporate practices that have tended to co-opt the value of ethical work (267).

In particular, Bietti notes that major players in generative AI development, including Google, Facebook, and Microsoft, have engaged in ethics washing behaviors through the development of "councils" or "boards" that ostensibly engage with ethical questions and harms related to their AI projects. However, rather than creating safety guardrails or protections for users, workers, and communities impacted by AI, these groups have tended to direct their attention to eliding many of the most complex ethical questions and problems. Instead, these boards provide cover for companies' ethically dubious choices, "allowing companies to 'ethics wash' their reputations and to narrow the space for real debate and change in AI" (Bietti 274).

Building on Bietti's articulation of ethics washing, then, we use the term "critwashing" to talk about a specific form of ethics washing where the rhetoric of critical inquiry, which is often used to question existing power structures, is ultimately used to reinforce the status quo, keeping those structures intact. Critwashing takes shape in the context of conversations about generative AI when appeals to diversity, equity, inclusion and access are used to advance adoption and uptake of these technologies while concerns about current harms including but not limited to environmental damage, mental health and cognitive impacts, linguistic homogenization, and global labor exploitation are short-circuited. As a result, critwashing co-opts the language of criticality to sanitize or make more palatable one's decision to use an oppressive, exploitative technology, and to quell fears of being accused of unethical behavior, whether those accusations are likely to happen. In some ways, critwashing parallels what Ben Williamson has called "critical hype," which includes "forms of critique that implicitly accept what the hype says AI can do and inadvertently boost[s] the credibility of those promoting it." In other words, the kinds of appeals to criticality that Williamson references tend to accept Big Tech's framing of the capabilities and impact of the products they're marketing to users and educators, while also advocating for prompt action, leaving less room for informed deliberation on the ethical, social, and environmental consequences of these products and technologies. Similarly, critwashing rhetorics result in the feeling that there are fewer options for responding to oppressive conditions than are actually possible, constraining individuals' choices to resist or otherwise act differently. Ultimately, we find that critwashing discourses tend to 1) center Western perspectives on---and experiences of---generative AI and its harms; 2) gesture to rhetorics of social justice and decolonization as ideals without corresponding actions; and 3) reinforce existing power structures under the guise of critique.

In what follows, we outline critwashing AI patterns used to justify generative AI use, adoption, and integration through appeals to diversity, equity, inclusion, and access while overlooking the realities of global oppression and harm. In short, the five critwashing AI patterns are:

1.  Generative AI is said to democratize writing, art, coding, and other technical skills, but who has access to this democratization is limited, unevenly distributed, and made possible through anti-democratic processes of exploitation and theft.

2.  Generative AI is said to help those harmed by linguistic oppression and racism, while it advances racist, sexist, and ableist bias and linguistic homogenization.

3.  Generative AI is said to be able to advance efforts to increase accessibility for neurodivergent and disabled writers and artists while taking away access from those who are already under-resourced.

4.  Generative AI is said to save time and labor, particularly for marginalized workers, but instead, it displaces time, including onto exploited global data workers.

5.  Generative AI and associated industries are said to provide important economic opportunities, but those opportunities are often false promises that rely on appeals to individual advancement over the well-being of communities.

We elaborate on each of these patterns below. As we do so, we respond to a number of stock arguments that are examples of critwashing, and we discuss how these arguments prop up the kinds of progress narratives necessary for the perpetuation of AI Empire. These stock arguments rely on the twin forces of hype and inevitability to paper over present harms, including the labor exploitation, financial abuse, and erasure articulated by Wilington, Yasser, Ruba, and Doe and Joe. In order, then, to more fully and fairly represent the present impacts of generative AI technologies and AI Empire, we articulate and challenge these common arguments through the identification of critwashing patterns.

## Critwashing AI Pattern #1: Generative AI is said to democratize writing, art, coding, and other technical skills, but who has access to this democratization is limited, unevenly distributed, and made possible through anti-democratic processes of exploitation and theft. 

One of the marketing promises of generative AI is that it will democratize writing, coding, art, and other creative and technical practices and literacies. We might ask, what does it mean for generative AI to democratize various skills when it is predicated on exploitation and theft of user-generated content, art, and writing scraped from the Internet without consent or compensation for authors, artist, and creators more broadly? It is true that writing and literacy have implications for democratic participation. Amy Wan has challenged any easy correlations between citizenship and democratic participation, on the one hand, and supposedly neutral visions of literacy on the other. As Wan makes clear, citizenship is always bound up in racial capitalism. Furthermore, appeals to democracy fall apart when we attend to the erosion of human rights and the exploitative, inhumane labor practices---laid bare by the narratives of Ruba, Wilington, Yasser, and Joe and Doe---required to uphold AI Empire.

## Critwashing AI Pattern #2: Generative AI is said to help those harmed by linguistic oppression and racism, while it advances racist, sexist, and ableist bias and linguistic homogenization. 

Alfred Owusu-Ansah, Antonio Byrd, and Carmen Kynard have argued that generative AI accelerates linguistic and stylistic homogenization. As Tacheva and Ramasubramania argue, "the fact that the language of AI Empire is English is not inconsequential; on the contrary, language is a cornerstone of a community's identity, shaped by and actively shaping its culture, worldview, and history" (6). Forcing people in colonized territories away from indigenous and local languages and into the use of standardized English---what Phillipson calls "linguistic imperialism"---is a colonial legacy dating back to the earliest days of the British Empire (see also Canagarajah and Said). In fact, Ngugi Wa Thiong'o argues, after the end of formal colonial rule, English itself remains as a disciplining structure that maintains the colonial order and preserves British and American power globally. This context is important to bear in mind as we think about the linguistic consequences of generative large language models (LLMs), which perform a similar colonizing mission as they homogenize language, further advance the dominance of standardized American English, strip away linguistic style and voices rooted in specific cultures and experiences, and ultimately narrow ways of thinking and understandings of history. The tech industry further strips away minoritized voices as it silences data workers like Ruba and Joe and Doe through NDAs, invisibilization, dehumanization, and erasure.

## Critwashing AI Pattern #3: Generative AI is said to be able to advance efforts to increase accessibility for neurodivergent and disabled writers and artists while taking away access from those who are already under-resourced. 

Scholars like Ashley Shew have interrogated how such arguments about generative AI and disability perpetuate ableism through technosolutionist discourses that present disability as a problem in need of fixing, as well as the privacy and surveillance risks associated with use in such cases. It is important to understand, too, that data work is often disabling, especially for data workers. As stories from Wilington, Ruba, and Doe and Joe demonstrate, data work is emotionally and physically taxing, due to both the content of their work and the pressures and precarity workers face. In another example from the Data Workers' Inquiry, Oskarina shared that a health crisis and hospitalization pushed her back into data work, which is to say that being disabled forced her into exploitative and emotionally and physically taxing labor. Additionally, scholars and public health organizations have written extensively about how climate crises disproportionately impact disabled individuals *and* are mass-disabling events (Engelman, Craig, and Iles). Because AI Empire relies not only on the exploitation of global labor but also the industrial practices that contribute to the climate crisis (Hao), we can see how AI Empire, too, is responsible for mass-disabling people around the world. The disabling effects of AI Empire happen at the local level---to communities whose health and well-being are impacted by neighboring data centers that power generative AI---and at the global level as the cumulative effects of increasing energy and resource consumption contribute to climate collapse.

## Critwashing AI Pattern #4: Generative AI is said to save time and labor, particularly for marginalized workers, but instead, it displaces time, including onto exploited global data workers.

The fourth stock argument suggests that workers, particularly marginalized or otherwise exploited workers, can use generative AI to save time and labor. This argument both erases the labor of data workers and hinges on increasingly dubious claims about generative AI efficiency. As Stuart Selber and Jason Tham have argued, generative AI doesn't save time; it moves it around, including displacing it onto exploited and underpaid data workers (Crawford). As is evident across all of the data worker stories, data work is extremely time and labor intensive, and data workers are often not compensated at all for their time and labor as their pay is routinely withheld for any number of dubious reasons. This is especially true for data workers like Yasser and Oskarina (another data worker writing for DAIR), whose work---and thus their pay and livelihoods---were disrupted by frequent power outages and grid strain.

## Critwashing AI Pattern #5: Generative AI and associated industries are said to provide important economic opportunities, but those opportunities are often false promises that rely on appeals to individual advancement over the well-being of communities. 

The fifth critwashing pattern suggests that generative AI adoption and integration will lead to important economic opportunities for low-income workers and developing economies and industries. For example, writing on the International Monetary Fund blog, Kristalina Georgieva argues that "the AI era is upon us, and it is still within our power to ensure it brings prosperity for all." Georgieva argues that "for emerging markets and developing economies, the priority should be laying a strong foundation through investments in digital infrastructure and a digitally competent workforce" to attract Big Tech investment. As the Data Workers' Inquiries make clear, however, these much lauded "opportunities" can be intensely exploitative, harrowing, and harmful. Each of the data worker stories we analyzed reveals how generative AI and other AI-driven industries rely on the exploitation of data workers. What's more, we hear echoes of the false promises of economic opportunity and financial stability made by the tech industry in each of the data worker stories. To make this appeal is to privilege individual advancement at the expense of global exploitation.

What we hope is evident after reading these data worker stories is that acknowledgment of harms is *not* enough. Each of the data worker stories we analyze above offer critical recommendations and solutions that would make their labor more ethical and their lives more fulfilling, healthy, and whole. All of the data workers---Ruba, Wilington, Yasser, and Doe and Joe---demand fair compensation and improved working conditions (including predictable scheduling, fair contracts, and safer working conditions). Ruba and Wilington also suggested that their work needed to be recognized and made more visible as a first step to regulating and improving their working conditions. The visibility of their experiences matters, both as a matter of fully understanding the ethical consequences of these technologies and as part of the global fight for labor justice. We must recognize, too, that each of these stories matters individually; moreover, in aggregate, they evince an extension of colonial rule that relies on extractive logics and devalues human dignity. In other words, the experiences described by the Data Workers' Inquiry researchers offer specific insights and pressing calls for change while also laying bare the ways that Big Tech has systemically harmed and exploited the same emerging economies, cultures, and peoples pillaged by previous forms of colonialism.

# CONCLUSION: AGAINST CRITWASHING PEDAGOGIES

The Data Workers' Inquiry offers important ethical and pedagogical lessons for the fields of rhetoric, composition, writing studies, and literacy studies. First, these stories are a reminder that the harms and consequences of classroom technologies are not imaginary or speculative; they are real and happening now, disproportionately impacting marginalized communities around the world. The labor exploitation laid bare by Wilington, Yasser, Ruba, and Doe and Joe in their inquiries makes clear the emotional, physical, financial, and psychological harm experienced by content moderators and other data workers, who are a necessary part of the ecosystem of social media, generative AI, and networked technologies. We also find these data worker stories to be particularly effective examples of rhetorically sophisticated, multimodal composition and therefore also important texts for writing and literacy classrooms. Not only do these multimodal narratives provide critical insights into---and calls-to-action for redressing---exploitation in the tech industry, but they also demonstrate the critical and rhetorical importance of storytelling as a mode of epistemic disruption.

Secondly, we see these narratives as an important continuation of disciplinary conversations about socially just pedagogies, which have a long and important history in writing studies and associated disciplines, from *Students' Rights to Their Own Language* to conversations about open-access admissions (Kynard, *Vernacular*) to calls for linguistic and racial justice in the wake of George Floyd's murder (*This Ain't Another Statement*). Similarly, disability rhetoric scholars in writing studies have articulated the need for accessible design and assessment, with the intention to increase inclusion and access for *all* writers in ways that do not erase differences nor norm writing processes for ableist workplaces (Hitt; Price; Dolmage). As a discipline, we need to recognize the inconsistencies between generative AI is and our values for language, disability, and labor justice. We contend that there's a need to seriously consider what message we send when we teach students about the environmental costs of generative AI during one class period only to encourage prompt engineering experimentation in another. We need to grapple with what we are really communicating when we discuss the need to forward Black linguistic justice (Baker-Bell) in our instruction and assessment in one moment before encouraging students to use LLMs for editing and polish in the next.

Ultimately, we need to understand these technologies not just in terms of individual affordances and decontextualized, technical capabilities but perhaps more importantly in terms of social, ideological, community, and global affordances and impacts (Byrd et al.; Selfe). We need to be cognizant of what Tacheva and Ramasubramanian describe as "the interlocking roots of AI Empire [which] are deeply steeped in heteropatriarchy, racial capitalism, white supremacy, and coloniality" (2). Ethical arguments for generative AI must account for the material realities and harms of the tech industry, and it is essential that these arguments do not erase or otherwise write off the lived experiences of data workers around the globe for the sake of "technological progress" (Tacheva and Ramasubramanian, 1). At the end of the day, we need to be honest if what we're invested in doing is making *some* international and U.S. white collar workers' jobs "easier" at the expense of the lives and livelihoods of marginalized communities and workers both domestically and abroad.
